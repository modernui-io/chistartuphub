## Zarminali Pediatrics — $110M Series A

Danish Qureshi built Zarminali after living through the nightmare of fragmented pediatric care with his own child. Specialists in one building, primary care in another, urgent care somewhere else entirely—parents left to coordinate everything themselves.

Zarminali puts it all under one roof. Primary care, specialists, urgent care, same records, same team. In under two years they've opened 28 clinics across 8 states. This round—led by Healthier Capital—funds 15 more, including Milwaukee and Dallas.

[PR Newswire](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/zarminali-pediatrics-raises-110m-series-a-to-transform-american-pediatric-care-302666472.html)

## Renterra — $9M Series A

The equipment rental industry runs on fax machines and spreadsheets. Nick Hibberd and Andy Feis built the software to replace both—quoting, inventory, dispatch, invoicing, GPS tracking, all in one platform.

Hundreds of rental businesses across the US and Canada now use it. Avenue Growth Partners led. Total raised: $16M.

[The SaaS News](https://www.thesaasnews.com/news/renterra-raises-9-million-in-series-a)

## The Blueprint: Braintree

If you've ever used Venmo to split a bill or paid for an Uber through the app, you've used Braintree. You just didn't know it. Braintree is one of those technologies that keeps on giving—invisible, essential, embedded in everything. And it started here. In Chicago. With $25,000 from a business plan competition.

Bryan Johnson spent his twenties selling credit card processing door-to-door in Nebraska and Utah. Not glamorous, but it taught him how money actually moves—at the register, at closing time, in the real world. In 2007, he enrolled at Chicago Booth, brought his startup through the New Venture Challenge, and won $25,000. More valuable than the money: Chuck Templeton, founder of OpenTable, became an advisor. That relationship turned into a $1.4 million contract. That contract funded his first engineering hires. Then came GitHub, 37signals, a small car service called Uber, and a home-sharing site called Airbnb.

In 2012, Johnson acquired Venmo for $26.2 million. Most people saw a feature—millennials splitting bar tabs. Johnson saw a behavior shift: people were becoming comfortable sending money through an app. The friction was disappearing. Thirteen months later, PayPal acquired Braintree for $800 million. Six years from a $25,000 prize to an $800 million exit.

The lesson: Johnson didn't chase big clients. He built infrastructure for companies that would become big. By the time Uber and Airbnb were household names, Braintree was already embedded. That's leverage.

### Why Chicago?

**The launchpad.** The New Venture Challenge gave Johnson his first $25,000 and his first advisor.

**The anchor contract.** That advisor—Chuck Templeton—gave him a $1.4 million deal with OpenTable. That deal funded everything that followed.

**The network.** Chicago Booth connected him to the people who could open doors that talent alone couldn't.

**The compound effect.** Braintree is now part of PayPal, processing billions annually. Venmo is a verb. And it all traces back to a business plan competition at Booth.

Chicago didn't just host the company. Chicago built the foundation. The ecosystem compounds. That's the point.
